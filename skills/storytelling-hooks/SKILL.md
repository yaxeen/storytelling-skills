# Storytelling Hooks: The Six Psychology Levers

- Short-form content lives or dies in the first 2 seconds.
- A strong hook stacks 2–3 levers in one line; a full script runs them in order.
- This is the **core skill** of the storytelling suite. Other skills (long-form-youtube, titles-and-thumbnails, carousel-narratives, email-hooks, wireframe-writer, wireframe-reviewer) recap these levers and link back here.

## When to Use

- Writing a reel / Short / TikTok script or opening line.
- Any niche or business: education, SaaS, fitness, finance, faith, food, B2B, personal brand.
- The moment must stop the scroll before the brain decides to move on.
- When a draft feels flat and you need to diagnose which lever is missing.

## The Six Levers (Quick Reference)

| # | Lever | One-line job |
|---|-------|--------------|
| 1 | Curiosity gap | Open a loop, don't close it |
| 2 | Emotional mirror | Target the feeling, not the fact |
| 3 | Conflict engine | Goal + obstacle = tension |
| 4 | Relatability | Broadest shared feeling → "this is me" |
| 5 | Pattern + surprise | Set expectation, then break it |
| 6 | Three-act structure | Setup → problem → resolution |

---

### 1. Curiosity Gap
- **What:** Open a question, don't answer it. The brain hates an unfinished loop.
- **Why:** An incomplete pattern creates tension; attention holds at the gap until it closes.
- **Move:** Reference something specific but unnamed — "this mistake", "this one setting", "the one habit". End on a cliff: "...and that's when everything changed."
- **Stronger:** Add a stake (reward gained or problem removed) → two gaps: "what is it?" + "what happens?"
- **Example:** "Most people lose money on this app without ever noticing the one toggle that's draining it."

### 2. Emotional Mirror
- **What:** Target a feeling, not just a fact.
- **Why:** Mirror neurons copy observed emotion; the viewer feels it as their own.
- **Move:** Swap dry information for the emotion underneath it.
- **Example:** Fact → "Here's a tip for staying productive." Mirror → "If your to-do list makes you feel behind before the day even starts, this is for you."

### 3. Conflict Engine
- **What:** A goal plus an obstacle. No obstacle = no tension.
- **Why:** The brain invests in unresolved tension: "will it work out or not?"
- **Move:** Name what's wanted, then what blocks it (a common mistake, a hidden trap, a force).
- **False obstacles:** The most gripping conflicts are often self-made — a fear or excuse the person creates. Applies to characters and to the viewer being addressed.
- **Example:** "You're doing everything right to grow — and one invisible habit is quietly capping your results."

### 4. Relatability
- **What:** Pick a feeling or situation many people share. The more universal, the more "this is about me".
- **Why:** People connect to what they see themselves in. Specific personal detail narrows; universal feeling widens.
- **Move:** Choose the broadest shared experience in your niche.
- **Trade-off:** Vivid detail adds texture but can shrink reach. "Waking at 4am in astronaut training" relates to no one; "waking up tired" relates to everyone. Detail for texture, universal feeling for the core.
- **Example:** "Ever sent a message, then refreshed your phone every 10 seconds waiting for the reply?"

### 5. Pattern + Surprise
- **What:** Set an expectation, then break it.
- **Why:** The brain predicts what's next; a wrong-in-an-interesting-way prediction releases a small dopamine hit and the moment sticks.
- **Move:** "Everything looks fine... but...". The word "but" is the switch. Surprise needs a setup first.
- **Example:** "She followed every rule in the productivity book. But the one thing that finally worked wasn't in it."

### 6. Three-Act Structure
- **What:** The skeleton of every story.
  1. **Setup** — normal world, everything ordinary.
  2. **Problem** — tension, mistake, or danger enters.
  3. **Resolution** — the outcome, carrying a lesson or feeling.
- **Why:** Gives the brain a clear arc (stability → disruption → release) that feels complete.
- **Example:** ordinary launch day → sales flatline and panic → the single change that turned it around.

## Building a Full Hook or Script

- Most short scripts are 3–6 lines. Run the levers in order:
1. **Open (1 + 4, often + 2):** curiosity gap wrapped in a relatable feeling. The scroll-stopper. Must work in 2 seconds.
2. **Build tension (3):** introduce the obstacle or common mistake; raise stakes.
3. **Twist if possible (5):** break the expectation you set. Optional but powerful.
4. **Pay off (6 resolution):** resolve with the lesson or emotional point.
5. **Close with a bookend** (below).

- **Worked example (stacked):**
  - "Ever feel like you're working harder than everyone but falling behind? *(relatability + mirror)*
  - Most people blame their schedule — the real leak is one habit they repeat every morning. *(conflict + curiosity gap)*
  - Fix that one thing and the whole day finally clicks." *(resolution + mirror)*

## The Bookend Technique

- Repeat a key phrase or number from the hook in the final line.
- Start and end touching the same phrase = the brain registers "completion" → satisfying, and pushes the viewer to comment/share.
- **Tip:** Concrete numbers and time gaps ("3 times", "in 7 days", "for 5 years") sharpen a hook. Reach for a specific number whenever facts allow.

## Pre-Publish Checklist

- [ ] Does line 1 open a curiosity gap, working in under 2 seconds?
- [ ] Is a real feeling targeted, not a dry fact?
- [ ] Is there a goal + obstacle creating tension?
- [ ] Is the core feeling universal enough for "this is me"?
- [ ] Is there at least one moment that breaks expectation?
- [ ] Does it move through setup → problem → resolution?
- [ ] Does the final line bookend back to the opening?
- If a line is flat, ask which lever is missing and add it.
